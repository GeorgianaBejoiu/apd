Pride and Prejudice by Jane Austen

It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife. 
However little known the feelings or views of such a man may be on his first entering a neighbourhood, 
this truth is so well fixed in the minds of the surrounding families, that he is considered the rightful property 
of someone. 

"My dear Mr. Bennet," said his lady to him one day, "have you heard that Netherfield Park is let at last?"
Mr. Bennet replied that he had not. 
"But it is," returned she; "for Mrs. Long has just been here, and she told me all about it."

Mr. Bennet made no answer. 
His wife remarked that he was so odd that she sometimes did not know what to make of him. 

Elizabeth Bennet had been obliged by the scarcity of gentlemen, 
to sit down frequently in the drawing-room at Meryton. 
Her sisters, Jane and Lydia, talked endlessly about balls and officers. 

Mr. Darcy was a tall, proud man, whose manners were considered arrogant. 
Yet Elizabeth found him intriguing. She observed him from afar, noticing every detail of his behavior. 

The day passed slowly, but it brought news to the Bennet household. 
Letters arrived, conversations followed, and the usual stir of the neighborhood carried on. 

Pride, prejudice, love, and family entanglements all combined in this story, 
which would become a classic tale of English society, manners, and morals.
